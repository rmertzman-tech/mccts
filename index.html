Purpose

The student’s reasoning process becomes the object of study

This Studio is not another empirical case site and not another full simulation engine. It is the bridge where students translate what they see and feel into public reasoning: claim, grounds, warrant, qualifier, rebuttal, repair, and PRF revision.

Phase S shift:

The recommended opening path now begins with Schelling-style emergence and Thompson’s problem of many hands, then uses those concepts to prepare the Challenger analysis.

Recommended Classroom Sequence

From emergence to responsibility to warning under pressure

1. Show emergence

Use the full Capability Lab Schelling simulation. Students see a harmful macro-pattern emerge from simple local rules.

2. Translate the OS1 reaction

Use this Studio to ask what the pattern makes students feel, notice, hide, claim, warrant, qualify, and repair.

3. Add Thompson’s many hands issue

Distributed causation makes responsibility difficult to assign, but not optional.

4. Apply to Challenger

Students then analyze suppression gaps, protected voice, uncertainty, and structural repair in a high-stakes institutional tragedy.

Thompson Bridge

From emergence to the problem of many hands

Schelling-style emergence helps students see how harmful patterns can arise without centralized intention. Thompson’s problem of many hands helps them see why responsibility becomes difficult in complex institutions: many agents, roles, rules, procedures, omissions, and incentives contribute to an outcome.

Key distinction:

Difficulty assigning responsibility is not the same as absence of responsibility.

Bad simplification 1: Hero / villain

“Find the one bad person and the ethical work is done.”

Bad simplification 2: System fog

“The system caused it, so no one is responsible.”

EBB alternative:

Trace responsibility through roles, warrants, decision points, suppressed warnings, institutional pressures, and repairable structures.

Responsibility Map

Five forms of responsibility students can distinguish

Individual responsibility

What did specific persons know, choose, ignore, or fail to ask?

Role responsibility

What duties came with a person’s position, expertise, or authority?

Institutional responsibility

What procedures, incentives, norms, or authority structures shaped the outcome?

Design responsibility

What rules, thresholds, access conditions, or decision architectures reproduced the pattern?

Repair responsibility

Once the pattern is visible, who must change the conditions that keep generating it?

Advanced Application

Challenger as the second demo

After Schelling and Thompson, Challenger becomes an advanced application of the same reasoning. It adds catastrophic stakes, professional knowledge, institutional heat, burden-of-proof reversal, and suppression of protected voice.

Bridge question:

When many hands are involved, how do we identify where warning, authority, evidence, and responsibility broke down?

Boisjoly / Engineering Safety PRF

felt certainty “This is wrong. We know enough not to launch.”

Notices: cold O-ring risk, expert warning, crew vulnerability, catastrophic asymmetry.

Mulloy / Launch Management PRF

felt certainty “We cannot stop a national launch schedule without decisive proof.”

Notices: prior successful launches, operational pressure, burden of proof, institutional credibility.

Use Challenger after students understand emergence, many hands, and repair responsibility.
Warrant Range Display

Different ethical lenses can warrant similar claims differently

Clarifying the warrant gap:

The demo may use one possible warrant, but the cards below show different ethical routes to a claim. Ask: Which warrant did my PRF reach for first? Which one did I not naturally see?

Many-Hands / Repair Warrant

Distributed causation does not eliminate responsibility once a harmful pattern is visible, repeatable, and changeable.

roles · design · repair

Epistemic / Suppression-Gap Warrant

When institutional authority systematically overrides domain expertise in safety-critical decisions, the institutional architecture bears responsibility for predictable outcomes.

knowledge · voice · authority

Kantian / Persons-as-Ends Warrant

Persons who may die from a decision cannot be treated merely as schedule variables, public-relations burdens, or operational constraints.

dignity · duty · ends

Care Ethics Warrant

Those with the most at stake and the least institutional voice deserve proportional moral attention, responsiveness, and protection.

vulnerability · response · dependency

Agency Loop

The two-sided model of agency

One side is lived phenomenal certitude: the world shows up as wrong, urgent, unfair, admirable, dangerous, or meaningful. The other side is uncertainty-aware modeling: claims must be public, evidence-sensitive, qualified, rebuttable, and repair-oriented.
